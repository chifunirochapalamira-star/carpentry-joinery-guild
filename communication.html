Introduction

Communication is an important element in life. Both animals and human beings need communication to survive. It is something we do all the time, mostly without thinking about it.

All too often there is a difference between what we say and what we think we have said, and between how we feel we have handled other people and how they think they have been treated. Hence the need for careful planning to communicate effectively when such ‘gaps’ occur.

Aims of Communication

Communication must have an aim or a purpose. The aims can be to:

  • Inform — the desire to supply factual information
  • Persuade — the desire to influence the recipient to accept a particular idea or opinion
  • Initiate action — the sender requires the recipient to perform a particular task
  • Enquire — to find out on the availability of a product, equipment etc.
  • Educate — give someone information about something they do not know
  • Express thoughts, ideas, opinions and feelings

The Communication Process

Definition of Communication
Before we discuss the communication process we need to start from the definition of the term communication. The word communication has been defined differently by different authors.

Key Definitions

  • the passing of information between two people
  • the art of transmitting or exchanging of information
  • the imparting or exchanging information
  • the transfer of information from one person to another through some medium
  • the process of sending or receiving information by using previously agreed upon symbols in a chosen medium in order to produce a desired response
  • the process of establishing commonness of thoughts between a sender and the receiver
  • a two way process of giving and receiving information through any number of channels
  • Communication is the imparting, conveying or exchanging of ideas or opinions using oral, written or visual forms.
  • Communication is a two-way process where information (a message) is sent from one person (the sender) through a channel to another person (the receiver), who reacts by giving feedback (the response)

Important: Communication is always a two-way process — there must be a Sender (transmitter/source/encoder) and a Receiver (audience/recipient/decoder).

Components (Elements) of Communication

(a) Information

Raw material (unprocessed data) that must be changed into a Message through Encoding.

(e) Encode

Process of turning Information into a Message so the Sender can choose the right Medium (Verbal, Written or Nonverbal).

(b) Message

Processed and encoded Information ready for the Receiver. A clear Message puts the audience in an understanding position.

(d) Sender

The deliverer who encodes the information and chooses the Channel. Sender can later become a Receiver when Feedback arrives.

(c) Decoding

The Receiver interprets the meaning of the Message (it may or may not match what the Sender intended).

(e) Receiver

The destination who decodes, uses or keeps the Message, and gives Feedback.

(f) Medium

The form of the Message:
Verbal: speech, lesson, conference
Written: letter, report, memo
Nonverbal: smile, nod, dressing

(g) Channel

The actual path the Message travels (telephone for oral, notice board for written, body language for visual).

(h) Purpose

The reason communication happens — inform, persuade, initiate action, enquire, educate, or express feelings.

(i) Feedback

The Receiver’s response that shows the Sender how the Message was understood (positive or negative).

(j) Motivation

The desire that turns the Purpose into an actual communication process.
